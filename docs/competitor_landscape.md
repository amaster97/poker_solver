# Competitor landscape — poker solvers, 2026

Personal-reference snapshot of the GTO poker solver market as of project start. Drawn from `references/` material (product docs, GTO Wizard engineering blog, six cloned OSS repos, PioSolver technical docs); this file is gitignored and not redistributed.

## TL;DR — where our project fits

We are building a **PioSolver-class local HU NLHE postflop solver** with a Python + Rust two-tier architecture: Python for tree configuration, scripting, range parsing, and UI orchestration; Rust for the hot CFR inner loop and hand evaluator. The target is a faster, cross-platform, scripting-first alternative to PioSolver, MIT-licensed, with a modern UI instead of Windows Forms. Multi-street postflop (flop / turn / river), no card abstraction, vector CFR, DCFR as the default trainer with CFR+ available, isomorphic chance reduction, configurable per-street bet sizing.

The competitive set splits cleanly into two clusters: **commercial desktop / SaaS** (PioSolver, GTO Wizard, MonkerSolver, Simple Postflop, GTO+, DeepSolver, Slumbot) and **open-source reference solvers** (postflop-solver, shark-2.0, TexasSolver, noambrown_poker_solver, slumbot2019, open_spiel). The commercial set defines what users expect; the OSS set defines what algorithms and patterns are publicly known and re-derivable. We aim to land between the two — modern OSS implementation, commercial-grade feature parity for HU postflop, no cloud library, no multiway claims.

## Commercial / closed-source

### PioSolver

- **Scope:** HU NLHE postflop only. Standalone Windows desktop. Single-machine, CPU-bound. Two-binary architecture: `PioSolver` (text-based engine, chess-engine-style stdin/stdout) and `PioViewer` (Windows GUI). RAM dominates — single bet-size trees 1.2–1.9 GB, multi-bet-size trees 5.9–7.8 GB, large trees 500 MB–4 GB on disk.
- **Strengths:** De facto industry standard. Fast on commodity hardware, deterministic, fully offline. Granular tree config (donk bets, custom raise sizes, accuracy targets, thread count). Resumable sessions. Scales linearly with physical cores. Sub-1% exploitability routine.
- **Weaknesses:** Windows-only (Parallels/Bootcamp on Mac). No multiway. No native preflop range-solving at scale. No ICM/bounty beyond manual rake adjustments. No web/mobile, no cloud, no precomputed library. Dated Windows-Forms GUI. Closed-source, perpetual license tied to major version.
- **Price model:** One-time perpetual license, tiered (Free / Basic / Pro / Edge). Edge adds VM/cloud-server support, script API, subtree solving. No subscription.
- **What we learn from it:** Text-based engine + separate GUI is the right architecture for scripting (chess-engine pattern). Bet-size DSL and accuracy target as user-facing config. Resumable solves via checkpointing. Per-physical-core scaling target — no GPU dependency. **Our differentiator: cross-platform Rust core, modern UI, MIT license.**

### GTO Wizard

- **Scope:** Cloud-hosted GTO study platform. Massive precomputed library (cash + tournament postflop) plus in-browser custom solver. Per the 2024 multiway-preflop launch, supports multiway preflop up to 9 players in the browser. ICM, MTT, spin-and-go, HU SnG, bounty in the library. Two automatic modes per the engineering blog: Fast Mode (shorter lookahead, large trees) and Classic Mode (street-by-street, better for nodelocking).
- **Strengths:** Library scale — millions of precomputed flop/turn/river spots at 0.1–0.3% Nash Distance, instant lookup. Cloud compute throws orders of magnitude more CPU at one solve than a local machine. **Hybrid architecture: CFR + neural-network value estimation** trained on billions of hands; "6s vs Pio's 4862s at 0.22% accuracy" benchmark (Pio on 16c/128GB vs GTOW on 2c/8GB). River spots re-solved in real time to 0.1% Nash Distance. April 2025 switched objective to **Quantal Response Equilibrium (QRE)**, hitting 0.12% pot average flop exploitability (25% better than previous 0.17%). Beats Slumbot by 19.4 bb/100 over 150k HUNL hands at 7s/move. Full study ecosystem (drills, hand-history reviews, spot-of-the-day, mobile UX).
- **Weaknesses:** SaaS lock-in — no offline, no local compute control, monthly fees stack. Custom spots still go through shared queue. **Multiway preflop is recent and theoretically fragile** — their own `gtow_quirks_multiway_nash` blog post admits "the foundational promises of a Nash Equilibrium don't hold" in multiplayer; the three-player Kuhn analysis shows EV-transfer anomalies. Ultra tier ($229–359/mo) is a hobbyist barrier. NN warm-start is a black box — users can't independently audit accuracy claims.
- **Price model:** SaaS subscription. Standard ~$50–100/mo; **Ultra** (custom multiway preflop) **$229–359/mo** depending on annual vs monthly and Early Bird vs Standard. Free tier limited to 10 BB stacks for custom solves.
- **What we learn from it:** Hybrid CFR + neural value estimation is the modern bar but multi-year infra we can't match. QRE is worth tracking as a future refinement. Their benchmark methodology (nodelocked flop strategies, 900 postflop spots vs PioSolver baseline) is the right shape for our own accuracy validation. **Their multiway claim is what we should NOT claim — theory is fragile and they own the only credible engineering effort.**

### MonkerSolver

- **Scope:** Multiway NLHE postflop solver, also handles preflop ranges for 3-bet+ multiway pots. Historical "go-to" for 3+ players postflop. Windows desktop.
- **Strengths:** Effectively the only off-the-shelf option for true multiway postflop. Handles ranges (not just hand-by-hand). Perpetual license, no cloud lock-in.
- **Weaknesses:** Slow and memory-hungry — multiway tree blows up combinatorially. Nontrivial 3-way turn spot takes hours on a 32-core box; 4-way+ impractical on commodity hardware. Uses heavy card-bucketing — solutions are per-bucket, not per-combo. Spartan UI. Windows-only. Infrequent updates. Likely loses share as GTO Wizard's cloud multiway matures into postflop.
- **Price model:** Perpetual license, ~€330 last published price.
- **What we learn from it:** Card abstraction is unavoidable for multiway — we explicitly do not go there in v0. Bucketed-strategy presentation is a UX challenge we don't take on.

### DeepSolver

- **Scope:** Newer entrant using neural-network-accelerated solving (similar conceptual approach to GTO Wizard's AI engine). Targets fast custom HU postflop solves at a lower price point than GTOW Ultra.
- **Strengths:** Modern NN warm-start without GTOW's full ecosystem overhead. Positioned as cheaper alternative for users who just want the solver.
- **Weaknesses:** Smaller market share, less battle-tested than Pio or GTOW. Closed-source. NN inference path means accuracy claims aren't independently verifiable.
- **Price model:** Subscription, below GTOW Ultra tier.
- **What we learn from it:** Confirms market direction toward NN-accelerated solving below GTOW pricing. Not directly threatening to our local / scriptable / MIT-licensed thesis.

### Simple Postflop / GTO+ / Wasilewski / Slumbot

- **Simple Postflop:** Russian-developed HU postflop solver competing with Pio. Similar feature set, perpetual license, smaller market share. Has a "Simple GTO Trainer" sibling and an integrated preflop solver (Simple Preflop Holdem) that handles multiway preflop better than Pio. Architecturally closest to Pio of all alternates.
- **GTO+:** Lower-cost ($75 one-time) HU postflop solver popular with hobbyists and low-stakes players. Solid accuracy, friendly Windows GUI, smaller feature set than Pio. **Closest to our entry price target.**
- **Wasilewski / PokerSnowie etc.:** Neural-net-based postflop strategy products that predate the GTOW-AI wave. Not pure solvers — closer to "GTO-leaning bot frontends." Limited overlap with our scope.
- **Slumbot (Eric Jackson):** Not a solver product — a fixed-strategy bot. ACPC HUNL champion, free to play via web/API. The canonical public reference opponent for HUNL strength claims (GTO Wizard beat it by 19.4 bb/100 over 150k hands). The associated `slumbot2019` OSS repo is the algorithmic substance; the product is just a hosted bot.

## Open source

### postflop-solver (b-inary)

- **License:** **AGPL-3.0-or-later** — strong copyleft. Read for reference patterns; do NOT copy verbatim unless we accept AGPL on the whole project.
- **Scope:** Pure-Rust HU NLHE postflop solver library. Multi-street (flop / turn / river), no card abstraction, isomorphism-aware. Backs the WASM Postflop and Desktop Postflop GUIs. **Same language and problem domain as our PR 5 — most directly relevant reference repo by far.**
- **Strengths:** Discounted CFR with custom params (γ=3.0, β=0.5, **cumulative strategy reset at every power-of-4 iteration**). Rich bet-size DSL (`60%, e, a, 2.5x`). PioSolver-compatible range parser (`"QQ+,AKs,..."`). Optional 16-bit compression (regrets `i16`, strategy `u16`, per-node `f32` scale). Bunching effect support (unique in OSS, slow but rigorous). Node locking. Rake support (`rake_rate` + `rake_cap`). Rayon parallelization at chance/action boundaries. Clean `Game` / `GameNode` trait split in `interface.rs`.
- **Weaknesses:** AGPL kills direct reuse. **Project is frozen** since Oct 2023 — author has publicly suspended OSS work to commercialize (see README banner pointing at issue #46). Heavy `unsafe` in hot paths (`get_unchecked`, raw `MaybeUninit::set_len`, `&mut *(... as *mut [f32])`) — unsound if misused. README warns of "breaking changes without version bumps." `custom-alloc` feature requires nightly and is one-game-at-a-time only.
- **What we learn from it:** **Architectural template for our Rust crate** — module split (`action_tree`, `game`, `bet_size`, `range`, `hand`, `solver`); `Game` / `GameNode` trait pattern that decouples solver from game representation; `Action` enum with chip-amount discriminants (concrete is easier to debug than abstract "raise-pot-X" tags).
- **Algorithmic patterns to re-derive:** DCFR γ=3 + power-of-4 strategy reset (non-obvious convergence win — paper says γ=2). **Inclusion-exclusion trick for fold-node CFV** — subtract per-card cfreach sums from total to handle blockers without scanning opponent range per player hand, O(N) at fold terminals. Range-aware isomorphic suit reduction at chance nodes. 16-bit compression with per-node scale. Slice-op modules (`fma_slices_uninit`, `sum_slices_f64_uninit`) for SIMD-friendly hot paths. Post-solve query API (`expected_values()`, `equity()`, `strategy()`).

### shark-2.0 (24parida)

- **License:** **No LICENSE file** — legally "all rights reserved" by default. README is permissive in tone but we treat as read-only reference; do not copy code without contacting the author.
- **Scope:** Actively-maintained C++20 HU NLHE postflop DCFR solver with FLTK GUI. Descended from Fossana's `discounted-cfr-poker-solver`. Uses Intel oneAPI TBB for parallelism and HenryRLee/PokerHandEvaluator for showdowns. **Most recently active of the six repos** (last commit April 2026).
- **Strengths:** Sensible defaults baked in — flop bet 50/100, turn/river bet 33/66/100, raise 50/100, raise cap 3, all-in threshold 0.67. Per-street action abstraction is simpler than postflop-solver's general DSL. "Force donk check" toggle. Range-aware suit isomorphism check at construction (more principled than purely geometric). int16 regret + int16 strategy with per-node `f32` scale. Periodic exploitability sampling (every `iterations/5` iters) + early-stop on threshold.
- **Weaknesses:** **No license.** Single-author project (college student per README) — bus factor of one. No automated test suite (only manual fixtures + benchmark). Aggressive `-O3 -ffast-math` flags can hide bugs. `thread_local std::vector` inside hot methods is fragile. `std::vector` allocations inside recursive traversal — measurable cost vs preallocated scratch buffers. README hints at historical correctness issues ("proper chance node updates" bug fixes). `PreflopRangeManager` doesn't actually do preflop solving despite the name.
- **What we learn from it:** **Sensible default per-street action abstraction** for our v0 config — fewer knobs than postflop-solver's DSL, lower surface area. Range-aware suit isomorphism is the right correctness criterion (collapse only if range distribution agrees under swap). Force donk check + raise cap → force all-in are easy tree-size wins. Periodic exploitability sampling + early-stop is simpler than per-iter checks. Separate `BestResponse` class mirroring CFR traversal but on average strategy. **Caveat:** prefer preallocated scratch buffers (Noam Brown style) over shark's vector allocations in Rust.

### TexasSolver (bupticybee)

- **License:** **AGPL-3.0** with **active commercial licensing program** (`licensed_list.txt` enumerates paying clients). Author monitors derivatives. **READ-ONLY REFERENCE — algorithmic ideas only, no code or paraphrasing.**
- **Scope:** Mature, production-quality C++ HU NLHE postflop solver (Texas hold'em + short deck). PioSolver-aligned output. Qt5/QML GUI, Python pybind11 bindings, OpenMP parallelism. Last commit March 2026 — but project has been superseded by closed-source `TexasSolverGPU`.
- **Strengths:** Two trainers (`cfr_plus` and `discounted`); DCFR defaults are paper values (α=1.5, β=0.5, γ=2.0). `Trainable` abstraction decouples per-action-node storage from solver — three storage variants (`DiscountedCfrTrainable` float, `DiscountedCfrTrainableHF` half-float via `half-1-12-0.h`, `DiscountedCfrTrainableSF` single-float). PioSolver-aligned output format for cross-tool comparison. **Tree-split parallelism at first chance node below root** (independent flop/turn subtrees on independent threads). Optional external-sampling MCCFR (`MonteCarolAlg::PUBLIC`). `use_isomorphism` flag with precomputed `color_iso_offset[52*52*2][4]` table. Warmup phase before main loop. Relative-%-of-pot accuracy as convergence target.
- **Weaknesses:** **AGPL + active commercial licensing program** — maintainer will sue derivatives. Codebase is "C++ port of Java port" — Java idioms (`shared_ptr<vector<...>>`, manager classes, JSON IO baked in). **Qt baked into the solver** (`PCfrSolver.cpp` includes `<QtCore>` and `<QObject>` for translatable progress strings) — solver is not cleanly separable from GUI in source layout. OpenMP-only parallelism. Single-author project effectively superseded by closed-source GPU version. CFR+ trainable variant is shorter than DCFR and may lag in correctness.
- **What we learn from it:** **`Trainable` trait abstraction** decoupling per-action-node storage from solver's recursive logic — let storage type (`f32` / `f16` / `i16`) be a config toggle, not separate solvers. Our Rust equivalent: `trait Trainable { fn current_strategy() -> &[f32]; fn average_strategy() -> &[f32]; fn update_regrets(&mut self, ...); }`. **PioSolver-aligned output format as an interop goal** — adopt similar JSON shape so users can diff against Pio output. Tree-split parallelism at first chance node maps cleanly to `rayon::join`. Per-street action-abstraction settings as a single config struct passed to tree builder once. Warmup phase as small but easy correctness gain. **Critical: don't look at the code while writing equivalent Rust — re-derive from Brown & Sandholm DCFR paper.**

### noambrown/poker_solver (Brown himself)

- **License:** **MIT** (Copyright 2025 Noam Brown). **Permissive — safe to copy / adapt.**
- **Scope:** River-only HU NLHE subgame solver in Python (reference) + C++17 (optimized). All major CFR variants — vanilla CFR, CFR+, Linear CFR, DCFR (default α=1.5, β=0, γ=2.0), external-sampling MCCFR. Kuhn / Leduc as validation games. Last commit January 2026.
- **Strengths:** **Single most copy-safe and algorithmically clean reference.** Vectorized river showdown: sort opponent strengths once, store per-player-hand `[range_start, range_end)` into sorted opp vector plus three blocker lists (`blocked_less` / `blocked_equal` / `blocked_greater`). Each `showdown_values` call is then O(|player_hands| × |blockers|) with prefix-sum lookups. **Per-depth `ScratchFrame` buffers — no allocations during traversal.** Single `Trainer` switches algorithm via enum. Python `vector_cfr.py` mirrors C++ for debugging. Subgame JSON format with `board`, `pot`, `stack`, `bet_sizes`, `include_all_in`, `max_raises`, `players[].hands/weights`. Action tokenization (`b300`, `r600`, `c`, `f`) for naming infosets.
- **Weaknesses:** **River-only.** No flop/turn dealing — extend tree construction yourself for multi-street. "Linear CFR" naming is non-standard (literally `regret_weight = avg_weight = t`, not Tammelin's CFR+ avg-weighting alone). Bet sizes are pot fractions of current-pot-before-calling for opening bets but pot-after-call for raises — subtle abstraction mismatch potential vs other solvers.
- **What we learn from it:** **Borrow the river vectorized eval verbatim (rewritten in Rust)** — sorted opp-strength prefix-sums + blocker partition is the right design and the bottleneck on river. **Per-depth scratch buffers** (`Vec<Vec<f32>>` indexed by depth, allocated once in trainer setup) — avoid per-traversal allocations. **Single `Trainer` enum-dispatched across CFR / CFR+ / LCFR / DCFR** with the same traversal code path; just change `regret_weight_`, `avg_weight_`, pre-discount scales per iteration. `f32` regret storage with `f64` traversal arithmetic — half memory at acceptable precision. JSON subgame config format — adopt directly for tooling interop. Exploitability via vector best-response with same traversal shape (swap `compute_strategy` for `compute_avg_strategy`, `max` over actions instead of weighted sum).

### slumbot2019 (ericgjackson)

- **License:** **MIT** (Copyright 2019 Eric Jackson). **Permissive — safe to port.**
- **Scope:** Research-grade C++17 poker CFR framework by the author of Slumbot. Distributed MCCFR / CFR+ over full multi-street Hold'em. Card and betting abstractions, subgame resolving (CFR-D + "unsafe"), multi-player support, disk-backed checkpointing. POSIX-only. Last commit September 2023.
- **Strengths:** **Most algorithmically deep of the six repos.** CFR+ (`CFRP`, "preferred algorithm when feasible" per README — regret floors 0, soft warmup, regret/sumprob scaling 16x default). External-Sampling MCCFR (`ECFR`, multi-threaded, samples one hand per player per iter). Targeted CFR (`TCFR`, ~2100 LOC production Slumbot trainer with compressed `unsigned char` / `unsigned short` regrets, pruning thresholds, boost thresholds, freeze streets). Subgame resolving (CFR-D, CFR-D combined, "unsafe"). Multi-player MCCFR. Explicit `Buckets` card-abstraction layer (`build_null_buckets`, `build_kmeans_buckets`, `build_unique_buckets`, `build_rollout_features`). Declarative betting-tree parameter files supporting reentrant trees. Per-street char/short regret quantization with precomputed `uncompress[256]` / `short_uncompress[65536]` tables. Worker-queue parallelism (`VCFR::SpawnWorkers` + bounded request queue + pthread cond vars). Disk-backed regret/sumprob storage enables solves larger than RAM. **Real-Game Best-Response (`rgbr.cpp`)** — exploitability in the actual game, not the abstraction.
- **Weaknesses:** Code style is dated (raw pointers, manual `pthread_create`/`pthread_mutex_*`, K&R braces, `fprintf(stderr, ...); exit(-1)`). TCFR is "a little messy" per README — ECFR is the cleaner reference. No automated tests (only manual integration drivers). `#if 0` half-finished distributed subgame parallelism in `cfrp.cpp`. POSIX-only (`drand48_r`, `pthread_*`, `sem_t`, `_GNU_SOURCE`) — won't compile on Windows without significant porting. Bucket files / hand-value trees are external precomputed binaries — workflow has a learning curve. `Game::MaxStreet()` and similar are file-scoped statics with global game state. **CFR+ in this codebase does NOT support multiplayer** (only MCCFR / ECFR / TCFR do).
- **What we learn from it:** **VCFR base class pattern** — share traversal / regret-update logic across CFR+, subgame resolver, and best-response via base class with virtual `OurChoice` / `OppChoice` / `StreetInitial`. In Rust: `trait CfrTraversal` with two impls (training vs best-response). **CFR+ as "preferred when feasible" default**, DCFR / MCCFR as alternatives. RegretFloors + RegretScaling per street + soft warmup (default 100 iters). `Asymmetric` flag to solve only one player's tree. **Regret compression with per-street quantization** — char-quantized river + short-quantized turn + short-quantized flop is known-good per `notes/lessons`. Worker-queue parallelism model (copy with `crossbeam` if rayon proves insufficient). **`notes/lessons` is a goldmine** — read for empirical findings: "Reentrant resolving seems like a good idea"; "Turn resolving > river resolving" for accuracy/cost; "Big wins the earlier the street you can resolve at"; "Approx RGBR doesn't do a good job on resolved systems."

### open_spiel (DeepMind)

- **License:** **Apache 2.0** — most permissive of the six. Free to copy / modify / distribute with notice.
- **Scope:** DeepMind's general-purpose game RL/search framework. Uniform `State` / `Game` abstraction + reference implementations of CFR, CFR+, DCFR, MCCFR (external + outcome), CFR-BR, best-response, exploitability, plus many RL algorithms across dozens of games (Kuhn, Leduc, Universal Poker, more). Last commit May 2026 — **most recently active of the six repos** by date. C++17 core, Python bindings, small Julia and Rust wrappers (Rust wrapper is **explicitly unmaintained**).
- **Strengths:** **Our correctness oracle.** When our high-performance Rust HU NLHE solver disagrees with open_spiel on Kuhn / Leduc, open_spiel is right. `CFRInfoStateValues` struct shape — `{legal_actions, cumulative_regrets, cumulative_policy, current_policy}` — simple, hashable, serializable. CFR / CFR+ / DCFR / LCFR share a base class with three booleans (`alternating_updates`, `linear_averaging`, `regret_matching_plus`). `AverageType::kSimple` vs `kFull` distinction for MCCFR average-strategy (correctness note for n>2 players — they explicitly call out Pluribus's choice of `kSimple`). `TabularBestResponse` with `prob_cut_threshold` and `action_value_tolerance` for partial / max-entropy BR. Universal Poker wraps the ACPC dealer. Serialization format with named sections (`[Meta]`, `[Game]`, `[SolverType]`, `[SolverSpecificState]`, `[SolverValuesTable]`).
- **Weaknesses:** **Performance is not the goal** — codebase favors generality and readability. `unordered_map<string, CFRInfoStateValues>` is fine for Kuhn/Leduc (936 infostates) but is the *wrong* data structure for HU NLHE postflop (millions of infostates). `State::Clone()` everywhere in algorithms — slow for poker. Big dependency: vendors abseil-cpp, PyTorch bindings (`dqn_torch`, `alpha_zero_torch`), `ortools`. Builds take a long time. **No vector CFR** — every solver loops per infoset/action, not over hand vectors. This is the fundamental difference between a "general game-solving framework" and a "poker solver" — for HU NLHE postflop we must do vector CFR (per Noam Brown / postflop-solver / shark) for speed. DCFR Python file warns it's unverified against paper results. `UniversalPoker` is via vendored ACPC C library — different code quality from rest of repo. Rust API is unmaintained.
- **What we learn from it:** **Use open_spiel as the regression test bench** — every PR (including PR 5) should run our Rust CFR on Kuhn + Leduc and assert exploitability matches open_spiel's `Exploitability()` to within tight tolerance (e.g. 1e-6 for Kuhn after 1000 iters). Adopt `CFRInfoStateValues` field names so cross-validation against open_spiel is mechanical. Mirror `CFRSolverBase` virtual interface as a Rust trait with `EvaluateAndUpdatePolicy()`, `AveragePolicy()`, `CurrentPolicy()`. Single config struct `{alternating_updates, linear_averaging, regret_matching_plus, alpha, beta, gamma, random_initial_regrets}` controls all of CFR / CFR+ / DCFR / LCFR. `TabularBestResponse` as exploitability primitive. Serialization format with named sections — adopt for our checkpoint files; add `[Meta]` so future versions can detect format changes. Use Leduc + Kuhn as v0 acceptance tests. `InformationStateString()` as infoset key during development; switch to dense integer encoding for HUNL once correct. **Copy only the small `cfr.*` files we need — do not link against open_spiel.**

## Where our project sits

- **vs PioSolver:** Same niche (local HU postflop solver), modernized. We win on cross-platform support (macOS / Linux / Windows from day one instead of Windows-only-via-emulation), modern UI built on current frameworks rather than Windows Forms, scripting-first Python tier on top of a Rust CFR core (vs Pio's monolithic C++), and MIT licensing. We probably lose on raw single-thread perf in v0 — Pio has had a decade of optimization — and we explicitly don't ship the trust / track-record Pio has accumulated. Acceptance test: solve a representative spot to 0.5% exploitability in comparable wall-time on the same hardware.

- **vs GTO Wizard:** Different product category. We do not compete on library coverage (millions of precomputed spots), cloud GPU pool, NN value warm-starts, ICM/MTT breadth, web/mobile UX, or study ecosystem. We compete on **local control, offline operation, scriptability, source availability, and no subscription**. A serious HU postflop solver user who wants to own their compute and audit their tool is our audience; a tournament grinder who needs ICM coverage is not. Their April-2025 QRE switch + 0.12% pot average flop exploitability is a target to track as a future refinement.

- **vs the open-source pack:** We are not adding another OSS solver from scratch — we are picking the best ideas from all six and assembling a polished, modern, cross-platform, MIT-licensed product:
  - **noambrown_poker_solver** → CFR engine reference, vectorized river eval, scratch-buffer pattern (copy-safe MIT).
  - **slumbot2019** → VCFR base class, RGBR exploitability, regret compression, empirical `notes/lessons` (copy-safe MIT).
  - **open_spiel** → regression test bench + `CFRInfoStateValues` field layout (copy-safe Apache 2.0).

## What we are deliberately NOT going to be

- **Not GTO Wizard** — no cloud library, no precomputed spots, no NN warm-starts, no SaaS billing, no ICM/MTT breadth. We are a focused local engine, not a study platform.
- **Not Pluribus / multiway** — no multiway equilibrium claim. The theoretical guarantees of Nash break down in n>2 zero-sum (per GTO Wizard's own `quirks_of_nash_in_multiway` blog post, and the three-player Kuhn EV-transfer anomaly). MonkerSolver and GTO Wizard's recent multiway preflop have the market; we don't claim it.
- **Not a research framework like open_spiel** — we are a focused HU NLHE postflop solver, not a general game-RL framework supporting dozens of games. We use open_spiel for correctness validation; we don't try to replicate it.
- **Not closed-source / not subscription** — MIT license, one-time install, no telemetry, no cloud dependency. Local-first.
- **Not Windows-only** — cross-platform from day one. Pio's Parallels-on-Mac workflow is a UX gap we explicitly close.
- **Not a bot** — we are a solver / study tool, not Slumbot. We do not ship a play-the-bot service or claim ACPC-competitive autonomous play.
